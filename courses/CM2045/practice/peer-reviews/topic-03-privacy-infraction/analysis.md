# The Clearview AI Controversy: A Privacy Analysis

Clearview AI is a US facial-recognition company that, by 2020, had scraped more than three billion images from Facebook, Instagram, YouTube, Venmo and the wider public web (a figure the company later claimed exceeded 30 billion) to build a searchable biometric database sold chiefly to law-enforcement agencies. A January 2020 *New York Times* investigation by Kashmir Hill brought it to public attention. The case is instructive because it shows how data that is technically "public" can still be processed in ways that are both unlawful and ethically indefensible.

## Nature of the infraction

Clearview violated nearly every core privacy principle at once. **Collection** was the central wrong: images and the faceprints derived from them were gathered with no consent, no notice and no lawful basis, in breach of the platforms' own terms of service. Because a faceprint is biometric data, it is "special category" data under the GDPR and requires explicit consent, which was never sought. **Usage** breached purpose limitation: people who posted a holiday photo never agreed to become entries in a police identification tool. **Retention** was indefinite, with no route for individuals to have their data erased. **Data security** mattered too: in February 2020 Clearview's entire client list was stolen, and unlike a password a face cannot be reset once exposed.

## Impact

The affected population is effectively anyone who has ever appeared in an online photo, billions of people who were never customers and never knew the company existed. The harms are diffuse but serious. False matches in policing risk wrongful arrest, a danger amplified by the well-documented higher error rates of facial recognition for women and people with darker skin (NIST and the Gender Shades study), so the burden falls unequally on groups already over-policed. More broadly, the tool erodes practical anonymity and chills lawful protest and free assembly. For Clearview itself the consequences were heavy: cease-and-desist letters from Google, Facebook, Twitter and LinkedIn, regulatory fines across multiple countries, bans on selling to private firms, and lasting reputational damage.

## Legal and ethical implications

Regulators responded forcefully. Under Illinois' Biometric Information Privacy Act (BIPA), the ACLU's lawsuit settled in May 2022 with Clearview agreeing to stop selling its database to private businesses nationwide. European authorities found breaches of the GDPR and issued €20 million fines in Italy, France and Greece, each with deletion orders. The UK ICO fined Clearview £7.5 million in 2022, though a tribunal overturned it in 2023 on jurisdictional grounds, an outcome that itself illustrates how cross-border enforcement struggles against a US firm serving foreign clients. Ethically, the case is a textbook example of the course's theme that legal does not equal ethical: even where scraping public images sat in a grey zone, public availability is not consent. It raises questions of autonomy, the power asymmetry of mass surveillance, and function creep as the tool spread beyond its stated policing purpose.

## Lessons learned

The clearest lesson is that "publicly accessible" does not mean "free to repurpose," especially for immutable biometric data. Organisations must establish a lawful basis before processing, apply data minimisation and purpose limitation, and honour subject-access and erasure rights by design rather than as an afterthought. Regulators have since codified this: the EU AI Act (2024) explicitly prohibits building facial-recognition databases through untargeted scraping of images from the internet. For individuals, tightening platform privacy settings and limiting public photo exposure helps at the margin, but the deeper lesson is that this is not a problem individuals can solve alone. It demands enforceable regulation and accountable corporate conduct. Clearview shows that when collection outruns consent, the damage is irreversible and collective, and that retrofitting privacy after a system is built is far harder than designing for it from the start.
