# Topic 10: Professional Development

> CM2045 · Topic 10 (Professional Development) · 6 lectures

## TL;DR
Treat your **career like a project**: set short- and long-term goals, audit your **career assets** (skills, experience, qualifications, networks), and reassess as your interests and the **volatile tech job market** shift. Your tactics depend on your **career stage** (starter, developer, changer). Clarify **career priorities** by aligning skills + values + interests against concrete tech **specialisations** (ML/AI, data science, web/mobile, IoT, game dev, VR, UX). Build a job-hunting toolkit: an **AI-optimised CV** tuned for **ATS keyword matching** but finished with the **human element**; a branded **LinkedIn** profile (photo, headline, about, experience, skills); a curated **GitHub** portfolio with strong **READMEs** and regular commits as "the new resume"; and a **professional network** built on **reciprocity** to reach the **hidden job market**. Throughout, AI is a **supplement, not a replacement** for human reflection, mentors, and real-world research, and everything ties back to **global employability skills** (communication, collaboration, complex problem solving, emotional intelligence, adaptability).

## 1. Strategic career development ([[career-strategy]])

Think and act **strategically** in a fast-moving tech sector. Take two views (likely exam fodder):
- **Longer view:** where do you want to be in **five years**?
- **Broader view:** beyond your degree, what enriching professional-development activities help you get there?

Addressing both makes your career path **resilient** in a dynamic market.

**Strategic planning = treat your career like a project** with goals, timelines, milestones:
1. Understand your **strengths, interests, experiences**.
2. Set **short-term goals** (gain relevant experience, certification, talk to 2-3 professionals, do a virtual taster/internship) and **long-term goals** (secure a role in your ideal field).
3. Map tasks/objectives that move you toward those goals.
4. **Regularly reassess and update** as interests or the industry shift.

Why it matters: employers value a **well-defined career strategy**, it signals **focus, self-awareness, and a proactive approach**. People with clear plans perform better, show more engagement, and adapt better to change.

### Review your career assets (likely exam fodder)
| Asset | Examples |
|---|---|
| **Skills** | Technical (programming languages) + **global employability skills** (communication, collaboration) |
| **Experience** | Internships, part-time work, freelance, side hustles |
| **Qualifications** | Certifications, specific coursework (add credibility) |
| **Networks** | Mentors, colleagues, peers (guidance + opportunities) |

Method: review **job descriptions** for target roles, **identify the skills you're missing**, then plan to close the gap.

### Career stage tailors your approach (likely exam fodder)
| Stage | Focus |
|---|---|
| **Career starter** | Build a solid foundation: hands-on experience via internships + project work |
| **Career developer** (some experience) | Advance by **specialising** or pursuing **leadership** opportunities |
| **Career changer** | Lean on **transferable skills**; seek roles/projects aligned with new goals |

Regardless of stage: **networking, skill assessment, continuous learning** are crucial.

### Resilience in a volatile market
- **Continuous learning** (courses, industry news, professional forums).
- **Skill diversification** (complementary skills: data analysis, AI applications, cybersecurity).
- **Strong network** (exchange ideas, learn future trends).
- **Embrace change**: view new tech as opportunity, not threat.
- **Global employability skills** (complex problem solving, emotional intelligence, collaboration) keep you adaptable even as technical skills evolve.

**Enrichment activity to prioritise:** internships / virtual taster experiences. Build skills, gain industry insight, grow your network, and create **proof of capability** (portfolio + recommendations).

**AI in career planning:** streamline job search (LinkedIn connections, CV-to-job matching platforms, AI learning platforms for skill gaps), but **critically assess** AI advice against mentors, experts, and personal research.

## 2. Career priorities and specialisations ([[career-priorities]])

**Key concept:** align your unique combination of **skills + values + interests** with specific tech roles. Employers favour candidates with **clear, well-defined goals** (signals commitment + long-term perspective); a focused candidate (e.g. targeting **IoT engineer**) beats a broad-but-unfocused one because they can **contribute immediately**.

### Questions to clarify your priorities (likely exam fodder)
- What areas of technology excite me most?
- Data, user experiences, or developing software?
- What problems do I want to solve? Creative freedom vs structured problem-solving?
- How much do I want to **earn**? How much **location/hours flexibility**?

Goal: make your career **satisfying** (aligned with interests) and **sustainable** (integrates with your wider life).

### BSc CS career specialisations (likely exam fodder)
| Specialisation | What it does | Typical roles |
|---|---|---|
| **Machine Learning & AI** | Algorithms to automate data analysis (finance, healthcare, entertainment) | ML engineer, AI specialist |
| **Data Science** | Collect + analyse data for informed decisions | Data scientist, data engineer, business data analyst |
| **Web & Mobile Development** | Build websites and apps | Front-end, back-end, mobile app developer |
| **Physical Computing / IoT** | Connect physical devices to internet (manufacturing, smart home) | IoT engineer, embedded software developer |
| **Game Development** | Interactive video games | Game designer, game programmer |
| **Virtual Reality** | Immersive tech across industries | VR developer, environment artist |
| **User Experience (UX)** | Human-computer interaction | UX designer, UX developer |

### Questions to test-fit a specialisation
What does a typical day look like? What problems would I solve? How much people-contact? What core skills are needed (and do I have them)? Which industries rely on it? What challenges do professionals face? What's needed for entry-level? How do I grow within it?

**AI use:** identify high-demand skills, analyse trends, suggest jobs from your resume, but **supplement, not replace** personal reflection; still talk to people in the field to check fit with your personality.

## 3. Enhancing your CV with AI ([[cv-with-ai]])

**Key concept: keyword optimisation.** Recruiters get hundreds of applications and rely on **ATS (Applicant Tracking Systems)** that scan for skills/experience/keywords matching the job description. Tailoring your CV to those keywords raises the chance of reaching the hiring manager (likely exam fodder).

It's **more than a crude word match**: strategically align skills with what the employer seeks and **back them with convincing evidence**. (Example: a job wants JavaScript, data analysis, collaboration → add a project where you used Python for data analysis and collaborated with teammates.)

**What AI helps with:**
- **Analyse** job descriptions, identify essential keywords, suggest natural ways to incorporate them.
- Provide **CV structure** (helpful for first-timers): personal details, career summary, education, technical experience, other work experience.
- Improve **spelling and grammar** (reinforces professional image).
- Suggest **wordings** for paragraphs / bullet points.
- Tip: **design your prompt** with detail about the intended reader and target job to get the right tone.

**Supports global employability skills:** digital skills + adaptability (optimising layout/content), complex problem solving + decision making (analysing role requirements), communication + creativity (a well-crafted CV), resilience (using modern tools).

### The human element (likely exam fodder)
AI is good at matching and enforcing conventional CV norms, but **doesn't know you**. Work **in partnership** with the AI:
- Give **specific detail** about achievements and their **impact**.
- Match how you work to the **values on the employer's website**.
- Aim for **at least three statements** that apply only to you, not to most people on your course. When everyone uses AI, those **human touches make you stand out**.

**UoL resource note:** specialist digital tools give personalised CV/cover-letter feedback and may beat generalised LLMs for this task.

## 4. Enhancing your LinkedIn profile ([[linkedin-profile]])

LinkedIn supports **every job-hunting phase**: exploration (discover paths, companies, connections), application (recruiters often check it **before** your resume), interview (reinforces credibility), networking (long-term support). Recruiters use it to gauge your **industry activity** (what you engage with, what you share, your connections) and **culture fit**.

**Key concept: branding.** Your profile is a **personal brand statement**. Aim for **clear, consistent messaging** so a recruiter instantly grasps your background, strengths, ambitions. Branding is about **how you want others to perceive your impact**.

### Profile checklist (likely exam fodder)
| Component | What to do |
|---|---|
| **Photo** | High-quality, professional, neat + friendly (no suit-and-tie required); your first impression |
| **Headline** | Current role or **target job title** + keywords. e.g. not "Computer Science student" but "**Aspiring software engineer, Python, AI enthusiast**" |
| **About** | Who you are + what excites you, technical skills, projects, goals; use **target-role keywords** for search visibility; ~**400 characters** display automatically, keep it succinct |
| **Experience** | Jobs, internships, freelance; if no formal job, use **academic projects** (technologies used, challenges overcome); **focus on results/impact** |
| **Skills** | List key languages/tools/concepts; get **endorsements** from classmates/colleagues; take LinkedIn **skill assessments** (Python, Java, SQL) to verify; include **global employability skills** too |

A polished profile signals you are **proactive and serious about your tech career**, and sets you up to build your network (Topic section 6).

## 5. Showcasing your work on GitHub ([[github-portfolio]])

GitHub is **more than storage**: a **social platform for developers**, a **portfolio**, and a gateway to the tech community. It's **"the new resume"**: recruiters check profiles to assess skills, and even **regular commits** show you're proactive (likely exam fodder).

### 10 tips for GitHub success (likely exam fodder)
| # | Tip | Detail |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Craft a stellar profile** | Treat it like a resume; keep it professional |
| 2 | **Showcase your best work** | Curate repos for impressive, well-organised, documented projects |
| 3 | **Contribute to open source** | Code, documentation, or bug fixes; shows skill + collaborative spirit |
| 4 | **Embrace the README** | Your project's storefront: what it does, how to use it, why it's good. Learn **Markdown** |
| 5 | **Master Git fundamentals** | Branching, merging, pull requests (essential for collaboration) |
| 6 | **Engage with the community** | Follow developers, star projects, join discussions |
| 7 | **Contribute to documentation** | Non-coders welcome: docs, tutorials, translation (designers + authors use GitHub too) |
| 8 | **Use GitHub Pages** | Build a personal site / portfolio; a lightweight static site is impressive |
| 9 | **Explore GitHub Actions** | Workflows from testing to deployment showcase **DevOps** skills |
| 10 | **Be consistent and active** | Regularly contribute, update profile, stay engaged |

**Real-world payoffs:** an impressive profile catches a recruiter's eye → interview → offer; open-source contributions earn recognition + conference talks (open-source companies hire too); team collaboration on a high-visibility project.

## 6. Building your network ([[networking]])

Networking connects you to advice, mentorship, and job leads. Many tech roles are **filled before they're advertised**, so networking taps the **hidden job market** (likely exam fodder). Employers value **referrals** (they come with trust) and see networkers as engaged, proactive, resourceful, with the **interpersonal skills** to thrive in teams.

**Key concept: reciprocity.** Networking builds **mutually valuable relationships**, a **cycle** where you help others and they support you. It's a **long-term investment** (you might work with a degree peer in 10 years).

### How to expand your network
- **Find a common link:** shared BSc CS programme, wider University of London / alumni connection.
- **Mutual acquaintances:** existing professional/family/social contacts can introduce you.
- **Industry events:** conferences, hackathons, discussions (regional + global online); UoL Career Service webinars/panels (engage in **live chats**).
- **Online platforms:** GitHub, LinkedIn, X, discussion boards; use **keyword searches**; invite a friendly contact to a **virtual coffee** (Zoom/Teams).
- Nervous? **Team up with a friend**; networking is **expected and valued** at events.

### Making a good first impression
- **In person:** do homework / research beforehand; know your **objective** (understand the industry vs get application advice); introduce your interests + why you're there, then ask an **open-ended question**; note their name, key points, contact details for follow-up.
- **Online:** engage actively in the chat box, respond positively, use person-to-person messaging to exchange contacts, follow up on social media.

### Follow-up questions to prepare (likely exam fodder)
What made you choose this career? Typical day? Useful background experience and how to get it? Where are vacancies advertised? What do you look for in a CV/application? Advice for someone entering this industry? Most important current sector issues? Individuals/organisations to know? How to keep up to date? Typical career progression? How will the job develop in future?

### Maintaining the network
- Send a **short courtesy thank-you email** with details from the conversation; keep them informed of your progress (especially if their advice helped).
- **Provide value:** share relevant articles, offer project assistance, congratulate achievements, engage with their posts, seek ways to **collaborate**.
- Networking develops + demonstrates **global employability skills**: adaptability (cultural norms), collaboration, communication, complex problem solving, creativity, emotional intelligence, and ultimately **leadership, persuasion, and negotiation**.
